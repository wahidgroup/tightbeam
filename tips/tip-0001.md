## Requirements Language

The key words "MUST", "MUST NOT", "REQUIRED", "SHALL", "SHALL NOT", "SHOULD", 
"SHOULD NOT", "RECOMMENDED", "MAY", and "OPTIONAL" in this document are to be 
interpreted as described in [RFC2119](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc2119).

## What is a TIP?

TIP stands for tightbeam Improvement Proposal. A TIP is a design document 
providing information to the tightbeam community, or describing a new feature 
for tightbeam or its processes or environment. The TIP MUST provide a concise 
technical specification of the change and a rationale for the change.

TIPs are the primary mechanism for proposing new features, collecting community 
input, and documenting design decisions that have gone into tightbeam. The TIP 
author is responsible for building consensus within the community and 
documenting dissenting opinions.

Because TIPs are maintained as text files in a versioned repository, their 
revision history is the historical record of the proposal.

## TIP Types

There are three kinds of TIP:

- A Standards Track TIP describes any change that affects most or all 
tightbeam implementations, such as a change to the frame structure, message 
encoding rules, ASN.1 specifications, security profiles, or any change or 
addition that affects the interoperability of applications using tightbeam.
- An Informational TIP describes a tightbeam design issue, or provides general 
guidelines or information to the tightbeam community, but does not propose a 
new feature. Informational TIPs do not necessarily represent a tightbeam 
community consensus or recommendation, so users and implementors MAY ignore 
them or follow their advice.
- A Process TIP describes a process surrounding tightbeam, or proposes a change
to (or an event in) a process. Process TIPs are like Standards Track TIPs but 
apply to areas other than the tightbeam messaging protocol itself. They MAY 
propose an implementation (outside tightbeam’s codebase), often require 
community consensus, and are more than recommendations. Examples include 
procedures, guidelines, changes to the decision-making process, testing 
frameworks, and changes to the tools or environment used in tightbeam 
development.

## TIP Work Flow

The TIP process begins with a new idea for tightbeam. Each potential TIP MUST 
have a champion — someone who writes the TIP using the style and format 
described below, shepherds discussions in the appropriate forums, and attempts 
to build community consensus around the idea. The champion SHOULD first 
ascertain whether the idea is TIP‑able.

Contributors SHOULD vet ideas publicly before drafting a TIP to save both the 
author and the community time.

Once initial viability is established, a draft TIP MUST be presented to the 
tightbeam community. This allows refinement to meet formatting and quality 
expectations and to address concerns about the proposal.

## TIP Formats and Templates

TIPs MUST be written in Markdown format. Image files SHOULD be included in a 
subdirectory for that TIP.

All TIPs MUST:

- Use RFC2119 normative keywords consistently to specify requirements, 
recommendations, and options.
- Adhere to tightbeam project nomenclature and terminology as defined in the 
repository (for example: tightbeam, “Frame”, “Metadata”, “Version”, “Message”, 
“TIP”). TIPs MUST use these terms consistently and link to or align with the 
Terminology in the [README](../README.md#2-terminology). New terms introduced 
by a TIP MUST be defined in a Terminology section within the TIP.

All TIPs SHOULD:

- Reserve RFC2119 keywords for normative statements and avoid them in 
non-normative rationale or examples.

## TIP Header Preamble

Each TIP MUST begin with an [RFC 822](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc822)–
style header preamble. The headers MUST appear in the following order. Headers 
marked with “*” are OPTIONAL and are described below. All other headers are 
REQUIRED.

```
TIP: <TIP number>
Title: <TIP title>
Author: <list of authors' real names and optionally, email addresses>
* Discussions-To: <email address or forum/URL>
Status: <Draft | Active | Proposed | Deferred | Rejected | Withdrawn | Final | Replaced | Obsolete>
Type: <Standards Track | Informational | Process>
Created: <date created on, in ISO 8601 (yyyy-mm-dd) format>
* Post-History: <dates of postings to mailing list or forum>
* Replaces: <TIP number>
* Superseded-By: <TIP number>
* Resolution: <URL to resolution or decision record>
```

## TIP Status Field

The typical paths of TIP status follow standard proposal workflows from Draft 
to Final.

- Champions of TIP MAY change the status between Draft, Deferred, or Withdrawn.
- The TIP editor MAY change the status to Deferred when no progress is being 
made on the TIP.
- A TIP MAY change status from Draft (or Rejected) to Proposed only if the 
author deems it complete, a working implementation exists where applicable, 
and community support has been demonstrated. A TIP editor SHALL review the 
TIP (with the help of a developer) and assign it an appropriate status.
- A Process TIP MAY change status from Draft to Active when it achieves rough 
consensus on the mailing list or forum. Rough consensus is achieved if the 
proposal has been open to discussion for at least one month and no person 
maintains any unaddressed substantiated objections. Addressed or obstructive 
objections MAY be overruled by general agreement that they have been 
sufficiently addressed, but clear reasoning MUST be provided.

## Rationale

The TIP process is necessary because the path of tightbeam standardization is 
decided by technical discussion and rough consensus, not by a small group of 
people working behind closed doors. The purpose of TIPs is to document 
decisions made regarding tightbeam, not to make the decisions themselves.
